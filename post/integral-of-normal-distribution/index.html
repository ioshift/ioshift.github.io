Integral of Normal Distribution

2023-12-29 3 min read # solved

Problem

Prove that

∫−∞∞e−x2dx=π\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2}\mathrm{d}x=\sqrt{\pi} ∫−∞∞​e−x2dx=π​

Solution

Let

I=∫0∞e−x2dxI=\int_{0}^\infty e^{-x^2}\mathrm{d}x I=∫0∞​e−x2dx

Consider

f(x)=∫0∞e−x2(α2+1)α2+1dαf′(x)=∫0∞e−x2(α2+1)⋅−2x(α2+1)α2+1dα=−2x∫0∞e−x2(α2+1)dαf(x)=\int_{0}^\infty \frac{e^{-x^2(\alpha^2+1)}}{\alpha^2+1}\mathrm{d}\alpha\\[1em] \begin{aligned} f'(x) &=\int_{0}^\infty \frac{e^{-x^2(\alpha^2+1)}\cdot-2x(\alpha^2+1)}{\alpha^2+1}\mathrm{d}\alpha \\[1em] &=-2x\int_{0}^\infty e^{-x^2(\alpha^2+1)}\mathrm{d}\alpha\\[1em] \end{aligned} f(x)=∫0∞​α2+1e−x2(α2+1)​dαf′(x)​=∫0∞​α2+1e−x2(α2+1)⋅−2x(α2+1)​dα=−2x∫0∞​e−x2(α2+1)dα​

let u=αxu=\alpha xu=αx, then

f′(x)=−2xe−x2∫0∞e−u2dux=−2e−x2∫0∞e−u2du\begin{aligned} f'(x) &=-2xe^{-x^2}\int_{0}^\infty e^{-u^2}\mathrm{d}\frac{u}{x} \\ &=-2e^{-x^2}\int_{0}^\infty e^{-u^2}\mathrm{d}u \end{aligned} f′(x)​=−2xe−x2∫0∞​e−u2dxu​=−2e−x2∫0∞​e−u2du​

Consider

∫0∞f′(x)=−2∫0∞e−x2dx∫0∞e−u2du=−2I2=f(∞)−f(0)=−π2\begin{aligned} \int_{0}^\infty f'(x) &=-2\int_{0}^\infty e^{-x^2}\mathrm{d}x\int_{0}^\infty e^{-u^2}\mathrm{d}u \\ &=-2I^2\\ &=f(\infty)-f(0)\\ &=-\frac{\pi}{2} \end{aligned} ∫0∞​f′(x)​=−2∫0∞​e−x2dx∫0∞​e−u2du=−2I2=f(∞)−f(0)=−2π​​

Thus I=π/2I=\sqrt{\pi}/2I=π​/2 and

∫−∞∞e−x2dx=2I=π\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2}\mathrm{d}x=2I=\sqrt{\pi} ∫−∞∞​e−x2dx=2I=π​

Comment

The method is similar to "Feynman's Technique of Integration", whose main idea is introducing another variable to help solve the problem.
